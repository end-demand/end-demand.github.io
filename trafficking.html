1. About Human Trafficking

Human trafficking is a modern form of slavery and a grave violation of human rights. [5] It is defined as the recruitment, transportation, harboring, or receipt of persons by means of threat, force, coercion, or deception for the purpose of exploitation. [3, 4, 5] This exploitation can take many forms, including forced labor, sexual exploitation, forced marriage, and organ removal. [10] Traffickers prey on vulnerable individuals, often profiting from their exploitation. [3] This crime can occur both within a country's borders and across international lines. [5]


2. Human Trafficking for Sexual Exploitation

Trafficking for sexual exploitation is one of the most common forms of human trafficking globally and in Switzerland. [17] Victims, predominantly women and girls, are forced into prostitution, pornography, or other forms of commercial sexual activity. [4, 10] Traffickers use manipulation, violence, and threats to control their victims. [28] In Switzerland, victims often come from Eastern Europe, Africa, Asia, and Latin America, drawn by false promises of a better life or a good job. [15, 18] The reality they face is one of debt bondage, confinement, and severe psychological and physical trauma. [22]


3. The Link between Human Trafficking and Prostitution

Prostitution and sex trafficking are inextricably linked. [12] While not all individuals in prostitution are trafficked, the vast majority of trafficking for sexual purposes ends in prostitution. [12] The demand for paid sex creates a profitable market for traffickers, who then supply vulnerable individuals to meet this demand. [12, 25] Legal or tolerated prostitution markets can expand the overall sex industry, which in turn can increase the scale of human trafficking. [16, 24] It becomes extremely difficult to distinguish between voluntary and forced prostitution, providing a cover for traffickers to operate. [16]


4. The System of prostitution

The system of prostitution is built on a foundation of inequality and exploitation. [30] It treats human beings, mostly women, as commodities to be bought and sold for sexual acts. [30, 32] This system is often controlled by pimps and organized criminal networks who profit from the exploitation of others. [28] The environment of prostitution is inherently violent, with studies showing that a high percentage of those in prostitution experience physical assault, rape, and suffer from post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) at rates comparable to combat veterans. [16] The system normalizes the idea that buying sexual access to another person's body is acceptable, which fuels the demand that drives sex trafficking. [25]


5. The Equality Model

The Equality Model, also known as the Nordic or "End Demand" Model, is a legal framework that addresses prostitution and sex trafficking from a human rights and gender equality perspective. [7, 11] Its core principles are:

  • Decriminalizing the prostituted person: It recognizes that those in prostitution are often victims of circumstance or coercion and should not be punished. [2] Instead, they are offered support services to help them exit the system if they choose. [9]
  • Criminalizing the buyers and exploiters: The model makes it illegal to buy sex and penalizes pimps, brothel owners, and traffickers who profit from the system. [6]
  • Reducing demand: By targeting the demand for paid sex, the model aims to shrink the overall market for prostitution, making it less profitable for traffickers. [6, 9]
Countries like Sweden, Norway, Canada, and France have adopted this model and have seen a reduction in street prostitution and human trafficking. [11]


6. Know the facts

  • In Switzerland, sexual exploitation remains the most identified form of human trafficking. [17] Between 2019 and 2023, Swiss police identified 295 victims. [17]
  • The majority of identified trafficking victims in Switzerland are women and girls (95% in 2019), many of whom are under 24 years old. [12]
  • Globally, an estimated 80% of all trafficking victims are women and girls, and up to 50% are minors. [16]
  • Research has shown that countries where prostitution is legal are associated with higher inflows of human trafficking. [24]
  • A study of prostituted individuals in nine countries revealed that 89% wanted to escape prostitution but had no other options for survival. [16, 20]
